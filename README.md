#  Saliva-Based Electrochemical Biosensor for Non-Invasive Glucose Detection

 A low-cost, non-invasive electrochemical biosensor for monitoring glucose levels in diabetic patients using saliva instead of blood.

---

# Overview

Diabetes affects millions of people worldwide, yet routine glucose monitoring still relies on invasive finger-prick blood tests that can be painful and reduce patient compliance.

This project proposes the development of a **saliva-based electrochemical biosensor** capable of detecting glucose concentrations with high sensitivity using enzyme-based electrochemistry and nanomaterial-enhanced electrodes.

The ultimate goal is to provide an affordable, portable, and patient-friendly point-of-care (POC) glucose monitoring system.

---

# Problem Statement

Traditional glucose monitoring has several limitations:

-  Painful finger-prick blood sampling
-  Poor patient compliance
-  Risk of infection
-  Expensive Continuous Glucose Monitoring (CGM) systems
-  Difficult for children and elderly patients

Saliva provides a promising non-invasive alternative because glucose concentration in saliva correlates with blood glucose levels.

---

# Project Objectives

## General Objective

Design, fabricate, and validate a sensitive saliva-based electrochemical biosensor for glucose detection.

## Specific Objectives

- Conduct a literature review on saliva glucose sensing technologies.
- Design a nanomaterial-modified electrode.
- Immobilize **Glucose Oxidase (GOx)** enzyme.
- Evaluate sensor sensitivity, selectivity, and detection range.
- Validate using artificial and real saliva samples.
- Compare results with commercial glucometers.
- Assess portability for future Point-of-Care applications.

- Linear detection range

- Sensitivity
- Selectivity
- Repeatability
- Storage stability

## Phase 4 – Clinical Validation

- Artificial saliva testing
- Human saliva samples
- Comparison with commercial glucometer
- Pearson Correlation
- Bland–Altman Analysis

## ⚠ Challenges

- Extremely low glucose concentration in saliva
- Interference from uric acid and ascorbic acid
- Variability in saliva composition
- Lack of standardized saliva-to-blood conversion models
